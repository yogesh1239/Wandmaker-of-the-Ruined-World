The timing was a little late to call it a godsend.

Still, the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm's disaster-reconstruction aid, delivered two months after the Tohoku Hunting Association's, looked like it could be a critical solution to the fire salamander problem left behind by the arson-sex culprit.

The circumstances were a little complicated.

Apparently, Hokkaido's large survivor community, the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm, had wanted all along to support Tokyo, which had gotten beaten up by the mushroom pandemic.

But it wasn't that simple.

For about three years, the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm had been troubled by a sea monster that ruled the coast like it owned the place: the Kraken.

Thanks to the Kraken, getting Hokkaido seafood had become insanely difficult. Every ship had been sunk, aquaculture farms had been destroyed, and people could not even safely build houses along the coast.

The Kraken was a pretty powerful monster that looked like a giant squid and octopus fused together, a Class A-2 by the Department of Monster Studies' classification. On top of that, it was exceptionally good at getting away. If it got injured or sensed danger, it quickly fled to the seafloor, escaping the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm's joint witch-and-mage hunting operations over and over.

The Tokyo Witches' Council might have been suffering from the mushroom pandemic's aftermath, but the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm was in the same boat. One witch in an important position had died in the pandemic. If anyone wanted aid, it was the Magic Beast Farm.

That was why reconstruction aid for Tokyo came with the condition that Tokyo hunt the Kraken.

Unless it got the Kraken killed in return, the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm couldn't agree to support the Witches' Council. It was strange that we had to give aid in order to receive aid, but it couldn't be helped. Everyone was having a hard time.

So the Tokyo Witches' Council chose the Mermaid Witch.

The Mermaid Witch had diminished intelligence and could not speak human language, but she was unmatched underwater. Once she picked prey, she could chase it down and kill it even in the ultra-deep sea. There was nobody better suited to hunt the Kraken.

The problem was her dolphin-level intelligence.

She was plenty smart for an animal, but it was hard to tell her that we wanted her to go kill Hokkaido's Kraken.

Once, she had listened eagerly to the request, smiled and nodded, and made us think she understood. Then she proudly brought back a giant squid that resembled the Kraken and tossed it onto the pier.

But the other day, we had finally managed to communicate with the Mermaid Witch. She smiled and nodded, then disappeared from Tokyo Bay for a full day.

Then, several days later, she came back as if nothing had happened, and an envoy from the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm arrived by sea aboard a sailing ship with a letter of thanks for hunting the Kraken and the aid supplies they had agreed on beforehand.

The documents listed everything included in the aid, and once I saw the contents, I understood that this wasn't something they could give away easily.

The aid was domesticated monsters.

In other words, what the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm called magic beasts.

There were three kinds of magic beasts, sixty in all. They had put together enough young pairs for long-term breeding.

The necessary raising techniques were also explained in detail, and the package included the necessary tools and feed, if only the minimum amount.

Basically, it was a Tokyo magic-beast livestock starter kit.

The Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm was not raising huge numbers of magic beasts, either. They were in the middle of promoting breeding and increasing their numbers, so sending sixty to Tokyo was a serious sacrifice for them.

But considering what they owed for fertility magic and the Kraken hunt, they decided the gift was warranted.

The Witches' Council was currently allocating stables and pastures for the sixty magic beasts. All I had on hand were the documents. Every magic beast was under the Witches' Council's control.

I was interested in Hokkaido's magic beasts, but I already had my hands full with the fire salamander problem. More importantly, know-how on taming monsters had landed right in my lap.

Could I use the monster-domestication techniques developed in the far north to tame the fire salamanders that refused to warm to me and just breathed fire and cried meep meep at me? I had pretty high hopes.

According to the valuable information in the documents, monster husbandry began with finding monsters that met the conditions for monster domestication.

The first was feed. Monsters that ate a lot or needed hard-to-get food were not suited to monster domestication.

The second was growth rate. Monsters that took twenty or thirty years from birth before you could get meat or fur from them were not suited to monster domestication.

The third was reproductive capacity. Monsters that needed vast territory to breed, or had abnormal reproductive behavior—like arson, for example—were hard to handle and not suited to monster domestication.

The fourth was temperament. Monsters that ate people or were aggressive were, naturally, not suited to monster domestication.

The fifth and final condition was forming a group with a hierarchy. When humans raised them, a human became the leader of the monster group, made them follow instructions, and could then raise them. Conversely, monsters that formed groups with no leader were not suited to monster domestication.

Everything it said made sense, and it was useful for my fire-salamander raising plan too.

The first condition, feed, was no problem. They ate small amounts of charcoal, so I wouldn't have trouble feeding them.

Their growth rate, the second condition, was unknown, but they didn't need to grow quickly. Considering the risk that they might emerge into humanoid forms, slower growth was better.

For the third, reproductive capacity, they went against the basic rule of livestock raising. Don't multiply, you guys.

Their temperament, the fourth condition, wasn't especially gentle, but it wasn't aggressive either. They got angry if one of their own was bullied, and they were wary of creatures bigger than themselves. It was a pretty normal temperament for living things.

As for the last condition, hierarchy, they seemed to have one, at least. Of the three fire salamanders, the slightly bigger, better-developed one often took the lead and scurried around ahead of the other two.

My overall evaluation was: “Problematic for monster domestication, but I could probably keep them as pets.”

For monsters that met the five monster-domestication conditions, the documents described the next step toward monster domestication.

You captured multiple monsters of the same kind, extracted the Gremlin from one of them, and implanted it in the caretaker's body.

Once the implanted Gremlin settled into the body, monsters with the same Gremlin began to recognize the caretaker as their own kind.

A caretaker who had a Gremlin implanted and blended into a group of monsters this way was called a beast handler.

It was a pretty gross modification surgery, but the benefits were huge.

Unless they were a species that fought territorial battles with their own kind or hurt each other, they would almost never attack you.

If you dealt with them properly, you could take the position of leader of the group and make the monsters obey you. At that point, monster domestication had succeeded, and a monster made controllable through successful monster domestication came to be called a magic beast.

But this modification surgery was not easy.

Implanting a Gremlin in a human body carried risks.

First was an allergic reaction.

Some people developed symptoms like a magical allergy when a Gremlin was implanted in their bodies.

Their magic power might stop recovering, their magic might always run wild, or magic might stop activating even when they recited an incantation.

Sometimes their bodies simply rejected the foreign object, and the skin around the implantation site became raw, throbbed painfully, or festered.

People prone to these allergic reactions weren't suited to being beast handlers.

Thankfully, a method to test for an allergic constitution in advance had been established.

You submerged the Gremlin planned for implantation in the blood of the person scheduled to receive it for about 12 hours.

If they had an allergy, a coagulation reaction occurred, and blood components clotted and precipitated. If no precipitate formed, there was no allergy.

Judging from the diagram of the allergy test in the documents, the blood formed a clearly visible clot that settled to the bottom. There was no way to mistake it.

But even after clearing the allergy problem, implanting a Gremlin came with a cost.

Permanent loss of magic-power capacity.

The implanted Gremlin took about one week to settle into the body. During that time, you gradually lost magic-power capacity while running a slight fever.

The magic-power capacity lost then did not return even if you dug out the Gremlin after it had settled into your body.

The magic-power capacity you lost was equal to the magic-power capacity of the monster that had possessed the implanted Gremlin.

That was why implanting a Gremlin from a powerful monster with powerful magic power was restricted almost entirely to witches and mages.

I wondered whether an ordinary person could implant the Gremlin of a powerful monster in exchange for losing all their magic power—whether they could command a monster much stronger than themselves—but no such luck.

Permanent loss of magic-power capacity brought irreversible death.

If Gremlin implantation made you lose all your magic power and reduced your magic-power capacity to zero, leaving you with no magic power at all, you turned to dust and dissolved into nothingness.

No corpse remained. You disappeared from this world completely.

It was irreversible death.

As a footnote, Professor Ohinata had written, “Magical death?”

I had heard about it before.

Magic had a concept of magical death in addition to brain death and cardiac arrest.

Professor Ohinata had understood magical death as a state where you lost your magic power and could never use magic again. That understanding was not wrong, but it was not precise either. If you turned to dust and disappeared from the world, of course you could never use magic again.

The fact that there might be a way to resurrect someone as long as they did not turn to dust and disappear from the world offered a glimpse of magic's deep potential. But with resurrection magic not confirmed to exist, brain death, cardiac arrest, and magical death were all the same right now. Death was death. Scary stuff.

You could implant a Gremlin anywhere, but normally it was implanted somewhere from the back of the hand to the upper arm. An implanted Gremlin also functioned as a magic-activation medium, so it was better to implant it in an easy-to-use body part.

Even if you took on those risks and went through all that trouble to implant a Gremlin, you needed to be fully aware that you had only reached the starting line of monster domestication—magic-beastification.

Implanting a Gremlin let you make monsters recognize you as the same kind as them.

But that was all.

It was easy to understand if you thought about humans. Would you feel affection for someone you passed in the street? Would you want to obey a complete stranger? Would you trust them with your safety and your life?

Of course not.

After Gremlin implantation, effort and know-how to build either a close relationship or a hierarchy with the target monster were essential.

The documents went into minute detail about the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm's know-how for raising the three kinds of magic beasts it had sent: how to establish a hierarchy, how to train them, how to prepare the stable environment, and what feed, temperature, humidity, and ventilation they preferred. It took up more than half the stack of documents.

How much trouble had gone into gathering that much information?

Even if they owed Tokyo for fertility magic and hunting the Kraken, I was amazed they'd agreed to share the fruits of all that work.

Because surely dozens—no, hundreds—of people had died before those documents were completed.

How many people had turned to dust before they understood the principle of magical death?

How many people had suffered from allergies?

How many people had been killed by monsters because taming had failed?

Thinking about that, the stack of documents felt heavy in my hands.

This stack of paper was made of lives. I appreciated it. And it terrified me.

Was it really okay for a mere genius Wand Maker who just leisurely made wands as a hobby in the sticks to shamelessly reap the benefits? I felt a little bad about it, but a sticky note in Professor Ohinata's handwriting was attached to the end of the documents: “It was possible to use implanted Gremlin substitutes made using melt-recast Gremlin coloring techniques.” That reassured me.

It had not even been three days since I received the documents from the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm, and they had already pulled off one applied study at crazy speed.

Sure enough, instead of going out of their way to dig a Gremlin out of a monster, they could draw blood from it and make a personal-color Gremlin as a substitute. And apparently, the substitution had actually worked.

If the technique I had developed could further advance the Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm's techniques, there was no reason for me to feel bad about it.

They probably thought they were sharing their techniques, only to get new techniques right back and fall over in shock.

Tokyo was amazing.

After all, we'd overcome trials just as hard as, or even harder than, Hokkaido, the so-called land of trials. Gahaha!

Anyway.

The Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm's documents were excellent from beginning to end.

Most of all, it was incredibly timely that they had arrived just when I was struggling with how to handle the fire salamanders.

Judging from the sticky notes scattered through the stack of documents, my magic-power capacity could endure Gremlin implantation from a monster around the middle of Class B-1. That was pretty excellent for a human, and considering Class A-3 or above was effectively for witches and mages only, I could not ask for more.

The fire salamanders were Class B-2. They were also juveniles that had only just been born. Even with a big margin of error, they were probably around the lower end of Class B-1, and implanting a fire salamander Gremlin would not turn me to dust.

In other words, I was qualified to raise fire salamanders!

I did not know whether monsters increased their magic-power capacity as they grew, but their capacity probably would not decrease. I should draw their blood early, make a fire salamander personal-color Gremlin, and implant it.

Strike while the iron was hot.

I waited until night and immediately visited the fire salamanders' nest.

Under the soft moonlight, the three fire salamanders slept soundly, curled up together in the middle of the metal nest inside the refrigerator. Well, well, they even had sleep bubbles puffing up from their noses.

Cute. If only I could've taken a picture...

But what I had brought today was not a camera. It was a syringe.

I wanted to draw their blood painlessly, but I didn't know how a fire salamander's body was put together. This might hurt a little, but they'd have to put up with it.

I silently crept up on the nest and gently inserted the needle between the scales.

“Mii!?”

The fire salamander jumped up in surprise at the sudden needle prick.

I quickly drew a tiny amount of blood and ran off as fast as I could before the groggy, confused fire salamander figured out what had happened.

Sooorry! But if I try to draw your blood in broad daylight, you guys will definitely breathe fire, right? An ambush was my only option. Forgive me.

I kept looking back to make sure the fire salamanders weren't chasing me as I returned home, then started up the reverberatory furnace and made a personal-color Gremlin mixed with fire salamander blood.

The finished oval Gremlin, about the size of a thumbnail, was blue like the fire salamanders—a blue very similar to the Blue Witch's personal color.

All I had to do now was implant this somewhere in my body and let it settle for one week. Then the fire salamanders would see me as one of their kind, and I would earn the right to take on the taming challenge.

If I trained them well and housed them in the furnace or kiln, I would have a legendary magic workshop where I forged magic items with the fire of magical life-forms. Seriously cool!

If I could prove that I could train and raise them properly, even the Blue Witch surely would not try to kill them.

Everything would work out nicely.

But...

In one corner of the workshop, I had fully prepared for self-surgery to implant the Gremlin. I had finished the allergy test too, but when it came time to cut into myself, I hesitated. I just could not make up my mind.

I mean, if I implant this, my magic-power capacity will go down.

And not just a little. I expected it to drop a whole lot.

This was not like the Gremlins from rabbit mutants or tanuki mutants. This was a Class B-2 Gremlin. And even setting aside the circumstances of their birth, it had been made from the blood of a supposed super-hybrid born between two witches.

Is it really safe to implant this Gremlin...? I was starting to feel like those little guys were more than just Class B-2.

What if, the moment I implanted it, my magic-power capacity immediately dropped to zero and I turned to dust?

Even if I did not turn to dust, my magic power would drop sharply, and my ability as a wizard would decline a lot.

I was a Wand Maker. I had no pride in myself as a wizard.

But I didn't want to get weaker. I mean, a path as a beast handler would open up instead... but... hmm...

Chickening out at the last moment, I thought about it for a while, put it off for the time being, and wrote a letter to the Flame Heir Witch.

Under the pretext that “there might be a problem with the sealing mechanism,” I asked her to send in Himori Wand, which held the sealed Flame Witch, for maintenance.

The Flame Witch was the one who should bear the most responsibility for the fire salamanders' birth. It was bad to wake her so soon after sealing her, but making her take responsibility was the neatest solution.

And since the Flame Witch was supposedly both their own kind (?) and their mom, she could probably train them without going out of her way to implant a Gremlin in her body.

But the reply that came back from the Flame Heir Witch the very next day said, “I'm worried about my big sister, so I'll accompany her for maintenance.”

O-Of course! That's what would happen...!

I wanted to refuse.

I wanted to tell the Flame Heir Witch not to come and to just send me the Flame Witch's wand.

But there was no way she'd agree to that.

If a dying family member was in cold sleep and the developer of the cold-sleep device suddenly said, “The machine might be bugged,” of course you'd worry yourself sick.

I could not refuse this request to accompany her. Refusing would look way too suspicious.

More to the point, the Blue Witch, who had delivered the letter, was already a little suspicious, saying, “It's rare for Ori to make this kind of mistake.” I screwed up.

I wanted to tell the Blue Witch and the Flame Heir Witch everything, temporarily break the Flame Witch's seal, and dump the fire salamander problem on the three people involved.

But I could never say, “They're the biological children from your unwitting arson sex. Acknowledge them,” or, “Your sister gave in to her sex drive, tricked the Blue Witch, and made these children.” It was scary to even imagine the mess that would cause.

Even if I tried to watch that nightmare situation from outside, I had gotten too close to the Blue Witch. It was not someone else's problem anymore.

I sent another letter to the Flame Heir Witch saying, “It was a misunderstanding. There is no need for maintenance,” then steeled myself and implanted the Gremlin in the back of my left hand.

For one week, I ran a slight fever like I had a mild cold and stayed quietly in bed to rest.

The Blue Witch tried to nurse me, but even though I was supposed to rest, she kept talking to me, wandering around the room, and sitting beside me being conspicuously there. The slight fever made it even more irritating. I just kicked her out.

I knew people with normal sensibilities wanted to be nursed. If a friend gets sick, you worry and take care of them.

But I did not want anyone to nurse me. I'm socially awkward, okay.

After one week, just as the documents said, my slight fever went away.

Now the fire salamanders should recognize me as one of their kind.

I didn't feel all that different. The Gremlin embedded in the back of my hand felt like a big scab, and the spot itched a little.

Then, for the first time in a week, I went to see the fire salamanders.

The three fire salamanders were scurrying around the burned-out site just as they had one week ago. When they saw me, they stopped dead.

But unlike before, they wagged their fire-tipped tails hard, ran over to me, and swarmed around my feet.

“O-ohhh...?”

“Mii.”

“Mi!”

“Mimimi!”

The fire salamanders grabbed my shoelaces in their mouths and tugged on them, while others tried their hardest to climb my pants with their little legs. It was hard to believe they'd once scattered sparks from their mouths to threaten me.

Th-They had grown attached to me!

They had grown attached to me so easily!

Amazing. It was like the accepted belief that monsters never warmed up to humans was a lie.

I crouched down and poked their red scales with a finger. They went meep meep and licked the finger that had poked them.

Even when I nervously cupped one in my palm and lifted it up, it didn't get angry at all. But its body was so hot I felt like I'd burn myself, so I put it down right away.

Now that the fire salamanders saw me as one of their own, even that seemed fun rather than scary. The other two tried to climb onto my hand too, like they wanted me to pick them up.

Wow. Once they saw me as one of their own, they were ridiculously friendly. Cute little guys.

There, there. Your big brother will take you to a new home, a new room.

From today on, you are members of the Ori family. Don't think of the Flame Witch as your parent. I won't hand you over to the Blue Witch either.

I'll take care of your food and home, so become the masters of my furnace and kiln and help me with my work.
