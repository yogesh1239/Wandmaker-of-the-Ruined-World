## English Localization

`Mita was working on producing unpronounceable sounds with musical instruments. If recording equipment still worked, he could record and replay those sounds and study them easily. But with every electronic device destroyed, nothing was that simple. He could hardly summon the witches and ask them to demonstrate the sounds whenever he needed a reference. To study sounds the human vocal apparatus could not produce, Mita planned to rely on instruments. He had already modified the guiro, a folk instrument used mainly in Latin music, to reproduce eagle-frog calls artificially and had successfully “performed” the frogs' primitive language. Sounds one mouth could not make could still be made by an instrument. It should therefore be possible to use instruments as substitutes for at least some unpronounceable sounds. If Mita's idea was correct, carving many grooves into a gourd and rubbing them with a stick would produce an approximation of one such sound. Many theoretical uncertainties and technical obstacles remained. His current experiment might fail, but he did not mind. Research moved closer to success each time an experiment failed, the cause was identified, and the method was improved. Seen that way, failure itself could be called part of success. As Mita worked in silence, occasionally rubbing his eyes, the laboratory door opened and a girl poked her head inside. She was Ohinata Kei, the only daughter of Professor Ohinata Soichi, who headed the laboratory. The professor doted on his daughter. He cooked up the excuse that it was too dangerous to leave her home alone in times like these and brought her to work every day. Kei displayed intelligence and manners almost impossible to imagine in an elementary-school student, and she quickly fit into the laboratory. Mita too had grown fond of the girl, who absorbed the latest magic-language research like a dry sponge. He stopped working and waved his lantern. Taking care not to catch herself on the boxes and research materials stacked high enough to narrow the aisle, Kei crept over to him. “Good evening, Mita-san. This is from Nagahori-san.” “Ah, thanks for bringing it. That helps.” Mita accepted several sheets clipped together from Kei and nodded gratefully. Nagahori was conducting an experiment that involved teaching a parrot a magic incantation containing unpronounceable sounds. Since Nagahori's field of research was close to Mita's, they kept up this close exchange of information.`
