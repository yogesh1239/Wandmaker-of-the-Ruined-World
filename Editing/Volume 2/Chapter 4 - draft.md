Top-class craftsmen had to be well versed in materials science in every age.

Carbon nanotubes, cutting-edge synthetic fibers, heat-resistant materials, and the like had brought innovation not just to industry, but to craftspeople who worked by hand too.

Long ago, blacksmiths set up workshops near mines and iron-sand-producing areas in search of good iron, while carpenters learned the properties of building materials and selected them to suit each building.

By understanding materials and using good ones, craftsmen boosted the quality of their work.

I was the world's most dexterous man, but when it came to materials science, I had to admit I was only a little better than an amateur.

Going forward, as the self-proclaimed one and only—and greatest—Wand Maker in the world, I had to become thoroughly familiar not just with how to process my materials, magic stones and Gremlins, but with their properties too.

I didn't let my guard down. Even after making Cyanos and Aleister, I still had a long way to go in honing my skills.

Even when I made money listing anime goods on online auctions, inferior copies of my work (apparently made with a 3D printer) had started circulating and taken market share from me.

Right now, no Wand Maker was better than me.

But if I got complacent, newcomers would copy my techniques, overtake me, and sneer, “That guy used to be amazing, but look at him now, lol.”

My manual dexterity wasn't something people could easily copy, but if some technological innovation brought electricity back and let people use the precision machinery gathering dust, things would change completely.

Never, ever let your guard down.

One autumn day, a year after the Dragon Witch kidnapped and imprisoned me, I finished the reverberatory furnace on the mountain behind my house and began a Gremlin-melting experiment.

Gremlins were important, readily available general-purpose materials that formed the core of magic wands. If I wanted to deepen my knowledge, this was where to start.

Being able to judge Gremlin quality with magic-excitation acoustic appraisal wasn't nearly enough. I wanted to deepen my knowledge more broadly and deeply.

I lit the reverberatory furnace, worked the bellows with my foot to send in air, and reread my notes to go back over this experiment.

It was well known that the bigger the Gremlin or magic stone, the greater its magic-amplification effect. But even the biggest natural Gremlins were only 40 mm in diameter. If I wanted a bigger mass, I'd have to make one artificially.

Even if I shaved down multiple Gremlins and fitted them together, or connected them with adhesive, they wouldn't count as one mass. So I figured, simple enough: if I melted a whole bunch of Gremlins at high temperature, then cooled and solidified them, wouldn't I get a giant mass?

Gremlins were milky-white crystals like quartz, so they looked like they ought to melt the same way quartz did.

But this was the kind of idea anybody could think of. The Flame Witch had tried it ages ago.

The Blue Witch had apparently taken part in the Gremlin melt-synthesis experiment conducted soon after the Gremlin Disaster, and she told me its results.

The Flame Witch's experiment had failed. Basic fire magic with low firepower hadn't melted the Gremlins at all, and when she used the fire magic with the highest firepower, instead of melting, they vanished into dust while giving off violet lightning.

From that result, researchers guessed that Gremlins, which grew by absorbing electricity, contained and stored the electricity they absorbed. Work had moved ahead on an energy experiment to extract and use electricity from Gremlins. But it remained suspended after Minato Ward's experimental facilities and research materials burned down and its researchers scattered.

It was a pretty interesting result, but failure was failure.

Still, as the saying went, failure was the mother of success.

The Gremlin experiment had been suspended indefinitely because Minato Ward was destroyed, but that same destruction had also brought a new fact to light.

Minato Ward had been destroyed when the giant kaiju came ashore last spring.

The buildings the giant kaiju destroyed caught fire, and with nobody to put the flames out, the blaze spread and turned Minato Ward to ash.

Then, one after another, Gremlins that had melted, cooled, and solidified were found in the burned-out wasteland Minato Ward had become.

Gremlins that should have given off violet lightning and turned to dust when exposed to high heat were somehow found in the Minato Ward ruins after melting once and solidifying.

I traced the reason to the difference between magical fire and natural fire.

For example, ice created by the Blue Witch's Great Glacier magic couldn't be melted by ordinary fire. Only magical flames could melt it.

In fact, even now, a year and a half after the kaiju died, thawing all of Hamura City frozen by Great Glacier magic had only progressed to about 50%, and the ice had resisted every attempt to melt it with anything but magical fire.

In other words, in magic, magical fire and natural fire were different things and behaved differently.

Naturally, the Flame Witch's Gremlin-melting experiment had used magical fire.

But the huge fire in Minato Ward had been natural fire.

The fierce heat from that massive natural fire had melted the Gremlins, which then cooled and solidified after the fire went out on its own.

This hypothesis seemed worth testing.

First, I tried melting Gremlins in a campfire.

This failed, as I'd expected.

A campfire only reached around 800°C at most. It could melt lead, but not copper, and not glass either. It couldn't melt Gremlins either.

If Gremlins melted in natural fire no stronger than a campfire, Gremlin melt-synthesis technology would have spread ages ago.

In this post-apocalypse world, where electricity was gone and survivors had long since used up the gas and gasoline, securing firepower greater than a campfire was hard work.

But this experiment was worth the trouble. If the result were unclear, that'd be one thing. But it had good odds of succeeding.

According to data I found after breaking into a fire station, a huge fire could reach as high as 1,200°C.

If Gremlins melted at 1,200°C, a climbing kiln used in crafts and such could reach that temperature. Building one would be a pain, but not insanely difficult.

But if I was going to the trouble anyway, I wanted enough heat to melt iron too. I had used up every iron plate of a usable size to reinforce the walls of the workshop the Dragon Witch smashed, so a furnace that could melt iron would be useful for making iron products going forward too.

So I built a reverberatory furnace that could reach 1,600°C, enough to melt iron as well.

A reverberatory furnace was an advanced design capable of some of the highest temperatures you could get without electricity or gas.

Securing the bricks to build the reverberatory furnace had been hard.

The Blue Witch had helped me with a lot more than making bricks, like casting Lost Mist over all of Okutama.

Lost Mist spread fog over a wide area and made any intruder other than its caster lose their sense of direction. But with the precise magic-power control only a witch had, she excluded me from its targets.

She even made sure the fog didn't cover the rice paddies and fields, so they still got sunlight. No, seriously, that helped a ton. Even if I used Lost Mist, I couldn't do anything that delicate.

I gave her a rare card that had once hit 16,000 yen at a card shop to thank her, but she hadn't seemed that happy about it.

Anyway, with the Blue Witch helping, I built the reverberatory furnace on the mountain behind my house using two references: old reverberatory-furnace blueprints, and a fanbook for a variety group that had built a new one in modern times. But a problem came up along the way.

I learned that a Gremlin that melted once and solidified lost its function as a magic-activation medium.

I found this out because the Blue Witch—who got stupidly loose-lipped, but only with Ome residents—told Professor Ohinata about my experiment, and the professor sent me a sample of melt-recast Gremlin collected in Minato Ward.

No wonder the Gremlin-melting experiment had been frozen. The research materials had been lost too, but more importantly, they'd gotten the result, “This seems kind of bad.”

Even if I melted small Gremlins, gathered them up, and solidified them into a giant Gremlin, it was only a milky-white crystal. You could cast magic right next to it and it wouldn't do a thing. It had become just a rock.

I got depressed when I found out the experiment would fail before I'd even started it, but I bounced back by setting a different experimental goal.

The Gremlin melt-dyeing experiment.

Gremlins were basically crystals that grew by absorbing electricity, but monsters had Gremlins too.

Unlike the milky-white Gremlins that grew by absorbing electricity, these monster Gremlins were colored.

For example, the Gremlin from the rabbit monster I got long ago was red. The white wolf monster the Blue Witch liked was blue, and Bigfoot was gray.

Colored Gremlins performed no differently from milky-white Gremlins, but just having color made them feel special somehow, and that was really nice.

Melting and recasting Gremlins would help get at the secret of these colored Gremlins.

Could I color them by adding a metal compound, like glass?

Or did I need to add a nonmetal compound?

What color would I get if I mixed a red Gremlin with a milky-white Gremlin?

There were all kinds of things I could do with melting experiments using the reverberatory furnace.

Learning all about dyeing Gremlins didn't have any particular benefit, but that was only true right now.

Research I couldn't see any use for right now might be useful as hell somewhere someday. Or it might never be useful at all. That was basic research—the kind of materials science a craftsman needed.

A craftsman with a mountain of interesting but useless knowledge gave off this real hotshot vibe. That was cool too.

Unlike Professor Ohinata, who could only do research that was “immediately useful” because her funding from the Foresight Mage—and by extension, the Tokyo Witches' Council—came with strings attached, I was free. I could research whatever I wanted for as long as I wanted.

Going solo had plenty of downsides and inconveniences, but at times like this, I was glad I liked being alone.

I reread all the research history and thoughts in my notebook, but the reverberatory furnace still hadn't come anywhere close to heating all the way up.

At this pace, I had to keep pumping the bellows and sending in air for another two hours.

Magical fire would've heated it up right away. But because of the nature of the experiment, it sucked that I had to struggle to raise the temperature with natural fire like this.

I kept working the bellows while wiping away sweat. This was tougher than I expected. I was gonna throw out my back.

Two hours later, exhausted after all that dull heavy labor, I finally confirmed that the Gremlins packed into the crucible inside the furnace had melted into a thick liquid, and I started slowly lowering the furnace's temperature.

Gremlins seemed crystalline, so they would probably crack when they solidified if I dropped the temperature too fast. I would gradually reduce the fire over time, then once it went out, seal the furnace vents with dirt and wait a full day for the temperature to come down naturally.

I spent three hours gradually reducing the fire until it went out. After a total of six hours tending it, I was wiped out and went home to take a bath. Between the heat, sweat, and soot, I was all smoky and my throat was dry. What a mess.

Throwing charcoal into the furnace to add fuel had been hard work too, in its own dull way.

Well, I got a good sweat out of it. If I did this job regularly, I might build some muscle.

The reverberatory furnace, which I had spent a full six months carefully building from the design stage onward, ran smoothly without any problems and brought me a huge amount of experimental data.

Bottom line, it looked like blood components from monsters were involved in coloring Gremlins.

A three-legged crow the Blue Witch hunted in Okutama had a light-green Gremlin at the base of its beak. When I mixed components obtained by boiling down and drying that crow's blood into melted Gremlins, they developed a light-green color.

Doing the same thing with ordinary crow blood components didn't add color, so some blood component unique to monsters was definitely responsible for coloring Gremlins (using powdered bone or skin didn't color them either).

While doing the coloring experiments, I also learned something that was all downside: if I wrapped an ordinary Gremlin in melt-recast Gremlin and used magic, the magic-power consumption doubled.

Seriously useless, but if I made a prison or something from melt-recast Gremlin, it might interfere with prisoners using magic. Maybe. Probably.

Magic-power consumption only doubled, so if they used twice as much magic power, they could cast magic normally. That made it pretty unreliable as a way to prevent magic.

Well, this experimental data would be useful someday too. Probably.

The principle behind coloring Gremlins wasn't useful for my main job of making magic wands, but it did help with my side job making accessories. Looked at that way, it wasn't bad at all.

Now then.

While I built a reverberatory furnace and played around with hobby research and processing that did absolutely nothing to help rebuild Japan's collapsed society, reconstruction kept moving forward in the cities, with the Tokyo Witches' Council taking the lead and people sometimes working together and sometimes not.

Of all the reconstruction news I heard from Professor Ohinata's letters, the thing that freaked me out most was that train service had resumed.

They weren't sleek metal boxes, though. They were freight trains made by linking together low-horsepower charcoal-powered vehicles. And they only ran on about 25 km of track repurposed from the former Tokyo Metro Marunouchi Line.

Even so, the return of public transportation gave the public a huge ray of hope. Professor Ohinata had attended the opening ceremony, and apparently half the people were in tears while the other half were celebrating.

For real, that was amazing. I got that it was possible in theory, but actually pulling it off in such a short time, with so few supplies and so little labor, was seriously amazing.

Bringing freight trains back only two and a half years after the Gremlin Disaster really let Japan live up to its reputation as a technological powerhouse.

Just as Tokyo had risen from the ashes after World War II ended, this convinced me it would surely rise from the Gremlin Disaster like a phoenix too.

The next big reconstruction story after the trains had to be the spread of fertility magic, which had kept moving forward since last year.

The fertility-magic bypass incantation had spread rapidly over the past year.

Mastering it was a matter of life and death, so more than 95% of Tokyo residents aged 12 and over had learned it.

They apparently made it a rule not to teach children 11 or younger, out of concern that curiosity might lead them to cast fertility magic on crops improperly, or that they might hit their heads or fall when they fainted from running out of magic power (Tokyo Magic University entrants were an exception, even if they were 11 or younger).

This year's food production had reached 98% of the needed amount, and estimates said that next year, along with expanded farmland, the food self-sufficiency rate would reach 102%.

That was an unbelievably rapid improvement, considering that through last year people had been crying, “Impossible. We're headed for starvation hell.”

But when I thought about it calmly, it was nuts that even with bugged magic that could instantly more than double crop yields, the self-sufficiency rate was still around 100%. Without magic, simple math put it at 50%. No wonder things would turn into a hellscape.

Even I, enjoying a carefree bachelor life out in the country, had been helped a ton when the yields from my fields and rice paddies doubled, so people in the cities who had been rationing food must've shed so many tears of joy they'd dried themselves out.

As a tech guy myself, I knew full well that brutal effort and impossible demands lay behind that flashy reconstruction story.

I manufactured and shipped general-purpose, mass-produced magic wands for Tokyo Magic University, which had started trialing a one-year curriculum, and in return, I got priority access to sugar, soy sauce, miso, seafood, clothes, other supplies I couldn't get easily on my own, and luxuries.

But Professor Ohinata had it way harder than I did. While teaching as a professor, she worked every day on recruiting staff and conducting research to establish a new department, all to make Tokyo Magic University the center of magic research. It sounded like magic wands were also very useful bait for recruiting people.

The magic wands were of unknown origin, but among Council members and knowledgeable people involved with the government, the leading theory seemed to be that a Wand Maker was being hidden in Ome.

The Blue Witch, who had the overtechnology Blue Wand Cyanos with nuclear-bomb-class amplification performance, made Ome her base and had laid out a solid defense system. And Professor Ohinata, the owner of the freakishly processed dodecahedral fractal wand Aleister, which looked like it could only have been made by inhuman hands, was also from Ome and had close ties to the Blue Witch.

There was also a rumor that the Dragon Witch had kidnapped an Ome resident, and the Blue Witch had torn off one of the Dragon Witch's legs.

Anyone who didn't suspect Ome after all that was an idiot.

Actually, I was the Wand Maker in question, living the easy craftsman life in Okutama next door to Ome. No way anyone could figure that out.

Thanks to the Blue Witch and Professor Ohinata protecting my secret and my safety (and the Dragon Witch being forced by the Blue Witch's threats to keep my secret), I got to live alone in this collapsed world just like I wanted. But I had a complaint too. Magic-wand making had been getting kind of stale lately.

For the last year, I had only obsessed over wand details, and I hadn't made any progress on improving performance or developing new features. I did occasionally run useless experiments, with melt-recast Gremlin at the top of the list, but I couldn't think of any good ideas to push wands forward. It was a kind of slump.

At times like this, the thing to do was ask customers for feedback.

I wrote to Professor Ohinata, a heavy user of magic wands, asking, “Is there any kind of magic wand you'd like to have?” I got a reply in no time, packed with detailed requirements for a backlash-prevention magic wand.

The request was more specific than I expected, and it got me interested.

At the cutting edge of magic research and application, it seemed my magic wands still had plenty of shortcomings.

Nice. This—this was what I wanted. A craftsman had to come through when a customer said, “I want this!”

All right! The next wand I'd make would be a backlash-prevention magic wand!
