After harvesting, threshing, and hulling the rice, I packed the resulting white rice into handmade straw rice bales, stacked them in the shed, and completed my preparations for winter.

Last year, as it got colder, I stopped catching fish and animals stopped getting caught in my traps, so I had to dip into my meager stores. It was nerve-racking, but this year was different.

I have plenty of rice. The Blue Witch will send some canned food and dried noodles my way too.

I can eat my fill every day without worrying about my remaining food. Can there be any greater happiness?

Good thing I took up rice farming. Good thing the fertility-magic bypass incantation was complete. I'm grateful for everything.

Still, there are things I should've done better. I doubled the rice harvest with fertility magic, sure, but it only doubled. It isn't like the quality went up.

The rice grains from my first rice crop are a little skinny. They're slimmer and smaller than the rice that had been on the market before civilization collapsed. The variety I'm growing is a standard one, so I probably didn't grow it right.

Obviously a first-year rice farmer can't make top-quality rice right away, but it still bugs me.

Also, getting kidnapped by a dragon had left me scrambling and pushed back the harvest schedule, so sparrows had eaten quite a bit of it. I'm gonna start hating creatures with wings. Unforgivable.

But it doesn't taste bad, and I'd grown extra expecting a bad harvest, so even after accounting for all kinds of mistakes, I've harvested more than enough to live on for the next year.

I caught fish and dried them, made smoked venison, and asked the Blue Witch to buy sugar and whale oil at the barter market. This year, I can sit back and watch the snow paint the mountains white from my window. Everything is completely different from last year's desperate early winter.

Since I had so much breathing room, I decided to invite the Blue Witch over. This year, I don't need to go into the mountains looking for scraps of food or force myself to sleep longer to conserve energy. I can just do what I like and have fun! That's how much breathing room I have.

“Hello, Blue Witch? Wanna come by my place right now? Let's play a card game!”

I called her through an eyeball familiar, and after a few beats, the Blue Witch's sleepy voice came back.

“...What? What did you say about cards? At this hour... The sun's only just risen.”

“A card game. I'll lend you a deck and teach you the rules. I told you about it before.”

“Hmm, a card game... You mean that thing where you summon girls and gems? My little sister played that, so I know a little about it.”

“That's Gemma. I play Gath.”

“Huh...? What's the difference?”

I listened to the Blue Witch's halfhearted responses as I carefully explained the history and different types of trading card games to her. Partway through, she cut me off, sounding exasperated.

“Okay, okay. I get it. I get that you really love card games. I'll humor you. Should I come over now?”

“Oh! Hell yeah. You haven't had breakfast yet, right? I'll make something, so let's play while we eat. Man, I've never hung out with a friend in my life. Hanging out with a friend is fun, right? I'm gonna see if the rumors are true.”

“Don't say sad things like that... I'll be there in 30 minutes.”

With that, the Blue Witch hung up.

Is there anything sad in what I just said? She's a weird one. I really don't get her.

I made three pickled-plum rice balls and set out some pickled daikon and brown-rice tea too. As I put charcoal in the kotatsu[^1], the Blue Witch arrived with snow in her hair.

It has to be cold outside, but the front of her black coat is open and her thighs are totally bare. Yikes, just looking at her makes me feel chilly.

“Hey. Aren't you cold?”

“Hm? Not really. If anything, weather this cold is more comfortable. I started liking the cold when I became a witch.”

“Huh. What about heat?”

“The same as anyone else. Oh, you got the kotatsu out? Can I get in?”

When I nodded, the Blue Witch shoved her legs into the kotatsu and took a breather. She might like the cold, but apparently she liked the kotatsu too.

The snow kept falling quietly, as though swallowing every sound, and time passed quietly while I spread cards over the kotatsu and gave her a beginner's lesson.

Over the course of the morning, the Blue Witch grasped the fairly complicated rules and learned to play matches using a simple deck.

This was where it got fun. When I pulled out a new deck to teach her special combos and technical plays, the Blue Witch went limp, tossed aside her cards, and raised both hands.

“Wait, wait. How much are you planning to cram into my head in one day? Give me a break. My head's gonna explode.”

“Oh, fair point. Sorry. I was having fun. I thought it'd be like playing both sides by myself, but card games really were made for two people.”

“Obviously. I mostly get the rules, but I don't really get what's fun about playing with magic, monsters, and life on cards when you can use real magic and there are real monsters.”

“Huh? You don't get it. It's because... Actually, why is it?”

Both of us tilted our heads.

Fantasy, once imaginary, had become real with the catastrophe. That had changed things for games, manga, and cards built around fantasy too.

I mean, chefs read cooking manga too. Even with the real thing around, people still loved fiction just the same.

“Is there anything that got more interesting because the world changed? Other than making magic wands.”

“That's just you. Hmm.”

As she gathered up the cards scattered over the table and put them away, the Blue Witch thought for a while. Then, as if she'd remembered something, she spoke.

“Monsters, maybe. They're a pain, but some are cute.”

“Ah.”

I imagined the Lovely Stoat Professor (beast form) and nodded deeply. True.

Professor Ohinata isn't a monster, but there's no question she's a blessing the Gremlin Disaster brought to the world, the incarnation of cuteness. Isn't her returning to human form a loss for humanity?

“Cute monsters are fun just to look at. Sometimes I just drive harmless cute monsters away.”

“What about cute monsters that are harmful?”

“I kill them.”

The Blue Witch's answer was short and merciless. Well, right. You throw killing intent around even when the other guy is human. Of course you do that. Sca-ry.

After lunch, we decided to spend the afternoon taking a walk to look for monsters.

We'd spent all morning playing the game I'd suggested and working our brains, so we'd spend the afternoon getting some exercise on the walk she'd suggested.

I bundled up in heavy cold-weather clothes and put on winter boots in the entryway, but the Blue Witch dressed light, not even changing her half-assed outfit that left her hot in summer and cold in winter.

I held up my spare fluffy earmuffs and waved them at her, but she raised a palm and firmly refused. You really don't mind the cold, do you?

“All right. Ready to go. Let's go!”

“Yeah. There's a cute monster you can only see in winter. Let's look for it while we explore Okutama.”

“Sure.”

We left the house together and headed out into the silver world lightly covered in snow.

We wandered along the snowy road beside the Tama River, but I saw fewer creatures than I'd expected. Well, it was winter, so that was only natural, but maybe because I had a ridiculously strong creature beside me, it felt like there were even fewer animals around than usual.

My breath drifted white in the air, while the Blue Witch's breath had no color. Small details like that reminded me that even if she looked human, she wasn't human after all.

After we'd walked for a while, the Blue Witch muttered.

“...This area really is peaceful. There are only weak monsters. Nothing tries to pick a fight when it sees me.”

“Yeah, true. I don't know if it's because of the area or what, but only monsters weak enough for Hendensho to chase off show up around here.”

When I waved my magic wand[Hendensho], the Blue Witch kept wandering along and tapped a branch of a deciduous tree bent under the weight of the snow with the tip of Cyanos, knocking a clump of snow onto the road.

“That's a good thing. It's better if there aren't any strong monsters.”

“But strong monsters give you good materials, right? We'd be in trouble if there weren't any.”

Monsters came in all levels of strength.

On one hand, there were super-weak slug monsters that were literally little more than slugs with hair and would die if you just stepped on them. On the other, there were disaster creatures like giant kaiju that would still cause casualties even if a bunch of ordinary Transcendents went after them together.

The Gremlins weak monsters had were small and poor quality.

The Gremlins strong monsters had were big and high quality.

To collect large Gremlins that made excellent core materials for wands, it was better to have strong monsters around.

When I offered my opinion as a Wand Maker, the Blue Witch threw the snowball in her hand straight into my face.

“Bwaa!?”

“Idiot. Strong monsters usually attack people. If you could get high-quality leather from them, would you allow a pack of man-eating tigers to prowl around your house?”

“W-Well, that'd be a little...”

“Right. It's true that you can get good materials from strong monsters, but it isn't worth it.”

According to the Blue Witch, who had lots of experience hunting monsters, there were plenty of problems with putting monster materials to use.

The problems with using monster materials could be divided into three major categories.

First, monsters were hard to defeat.

The monsters I'd fought to the death in Okutama with Hendensho in one hand counted as small fry. In central Tokyo, monsters many times stronger than the ones I'd struggled to chase off showed up all over the place.

Man-eating monsters popped up in urban areas like clockwork, so of course people died. At present, only witches and mages could defeat monsters above a certain level of strength.

Second, monster meat couldn't be eaten.

There were all kinds of monsters—chimera type, beast type, plant type, ghost type—but none of them were edible. They didn't taste bad, and some were even tasty, but eating them upset your stomach.

I knew that well because I'd tried to find some way to eat it and thrown up over and over. You couldn't eat monster meat, no matter whether you boiled it or grilled it.

On top of that, monsters tore up fields and crops just like wild boars and deer, which made them a pain.

You'd kill some obnoxious monster that had stuffed itself fat on vegetables you'd carefully raised, only to find you couldn't even turn it into food and take back the calories. They really pissed me off.

The exception was witches and mages, who could eat monster meat without any trouble.

Monsters also seemed fine eating each other according to the law of the jungle, so Transcendents, who had mutated from humans and become inhuman, were probably physiologically closer to monsters.

The final problem was material deterioration.

Monster materials were better than ordinary animal materials.

Their claws were sharp, their hides were tough, and their bones were sturdy.

You could even get materials with strange, magical properties you'd never expect from their appearance.

But those high-performance materials deteriorated over time. Magic power was the cause.

Monsters were magical by nature, so they had magic power in them.

Even after they died, magic power remained in their corpses, letting monster materials do things that seemed like magic.

But that also meant their special properties vanished if the magic power did.

All living things gradually lost magic power after they died. As they lost magic power, their performance deteriorated too.

Depending on the material, it lost its magic power and completely deteriorated in as little as a few days, or one month at most.

Bones as strong as steel became ordinary bones, and brilliantly glowing feathers lost their light and became ordinary feathers.

Even if you risked your life to defeat a monster, you couldn't eat its meat, and the remaining materials quickly deteriorated until they were useless. That wasn't worth it at all.

I got sick of listening to the Blue Witch go on and on about “Everything Wrong with Monster Materials!”

They're pests while they're alive, and killing them has no upside. Monsters are totally hopeless.

“So killing monsters doesn't do you any good...”

“That's right. But Ori changed things a little.”

“Me?”

Confused, I pointed at myself. The Blue Witch gave a little smile.

“Of all the materials you can get from monsters, Gremlins alone don't deteriorate over time. Gremlins were too difficult to process to be useful, but Ori can process them. Only Ori can process them. You can draw out their capabilities and turn them into magic wands.”

“Oh! No, you're right. That's right. That frozen giant kaiju you killed—once it finishes thawing, we can probably pull a fucking huge Gremlin out of it. Let's keep taking down strong monsters and grabbing huge Gremlins! We'll make tons of magic wands!”

“We're the ones risking our lives to fight the monsters for that.”

“...Sorry.”

In the end, it comes back to that.

There are things to gain from monsters. But they aren't worth the risk.

That's the point.

For the sake of the people risking their lives to fight monsters, I have to make even higher-performance magic wands.

Make better wands.

Have them use those to defeat stronger monsters.

Get higher-quality Gremlins.

Use those Gremlins to make even better wands.

It'd be great if we could get a cycle like that going.

We'd gone pretty far while we talked, and Lake Okutama came into view. The mountains and forest had hemmed in our view, but now it opened up wide, with thick clouds visible in the distance. If we didn't turn back soon, it looked like we'd get caught in heavy snow.

We still hadn't found the cute monster the Blue Witch was after.

“What kind of monster are we looking for in the first place?”

“It's a fluffy white wolf-like thing that only shows up when there's snow on the ground. It has a blue Gremlin on its forehead. It probably mutated from someone's pet dog or something. It's pretty tame for a monster and doesn't attack people.”

“Huh.”

“I want to touch it, but it runs away if I get too close. All I can do is look.”

The Blue Witch clenched and unclenched both hands wistfully, then sighed.

This girl really likes animals. I get the stoat thing, but are wolves cute...? Aren't they more cool than cute?

We walked along the shore of Lake Okutama for a while, but we didn't see the white wolf. All we found was a turtle monster with an absurdly long neck swimming through ripples kicked up by the cold wind. It was kind of funny seeing several little turtles looking around from on top of its huge 2 m shell. Are they a parent and its kids?

I wished monsters would quit reminding me that they weren't mysterious horrors, but living creatures doing their best to get by every day. That just made them harder to kill.

As we walked and chatted off and on, the sun started to set and it got colder. The snowflakes drifting down grew bigger and heavier. It was starting to feel like time to pack it in.

“Hey, let's head back soon. We can come again tomorrow.”

“Mm... A little longer. I feel like we'll find it if we look just a little longer.”

“Really? Ah!?”

I was idly looking around where the road met the mountain and wondering if I should just ditch the Blue Witch and head home alone when I spotted something unbelievable and yelled.

Footprints.

Footprints were pressed into the fresh snow.

In shape, they looked like ordinary barefoot human tracks, but they were an estimated 40 cm long.

“W-What!? Bigfoot!? It's real!?”

“What is it? What did you find?”

This totally unexpected discovery sent adrenaline racing through my brain. As I knelt on the snow and examined the trail of giant footprints leading deep into the mountains, the Blue Witch leaned in from beside me and recoiled.

“Whoa! W-What is this? Human footprints... but they're way too big.”

“I'm telling you, it's Bigfoot. Never heard of it?”

“What is that? A monster? Not a giant?”

The Blue Witch looked creeped out, so I traced a giant footprint with my finger and explained.

Bigfoot was an unidentified mysterious animal[Unidentified Mysterious Animal]—a UMA for short—made famous by the occult boom at the end of the twentieth century. Nessie, Tsuchinoko, Chupacabra—all those gross-cute creatures that looked like laid-back mascots used to be big names back then.

Bigfoot was a hairy giant that lived deep in the mountains and, true to its name, was famous for leaving huge footprints. It was said to be either a surviving ape-man or a prehistoric human whose lineage had split off from humanity. That just-plausible-enough backstory had always captured my imagination.

But even after I told her all about Bigfoot, the Blue Witch was skeptical.

“So it's a humanoid monster, right? I don't think something like this was on Earth before the Gremlin Disaster.”

“No, no, who knows? Maybe monsters had been hiding on Earth all along, and the Gremlin Disaster made them show themselves.”

Like kappa, karakasa-obake, and stuff. Who can say that old-school, homegrown supernatural beings aren't mixed in among the monsters running rampant?

As I argued passionately enough to melt the piled-up snow, the Blue Witch seemed to get into it too. She leaned forward, looking excited.

“Then nekomata, the nine-tailed fox, and kamaitachi[^2] could be real too...?”

“You like fluffy creatures way too much. I'd say there's a chance.”

The Blue Witch was suddenly all in, so the two of us started tracking the footprints of our provisional Bigfoot.

Hell yeah! We're UMA hunters!

If we can prove Bigfoot is real, there'll be hope that other UMAs and yokai are real too.

This could be the discovery of a new species with a huge impact on both monster studies and biology!

The giant footprints wove between the trees as they led deeper and deeper into the mountain. They were pressed so deeply into the snow that there was no way to lose them.

The Blue Witch led the way along the footprints. She glanced back, waited for me to catch up, and asked,

“What'll you do if all we find at the end of these tracks is a normal old guy with ridiculously huge feet?”

“That'd be fine too. If an old guy with 40 cm feet was walking barefoot through a snowy mountain like this, that'd be pretty funny.”

“...True.”

I hate meeting people, but if someone is that weird, I kind of want to see him just once.

Finding Bigfoot would be good. Finding a weird old guy would be good too.

Either way, this expedition is a guaranteed win.

The snowfall gradually got heavier, and a blizzard seemed likely. But before the snow completely blocked our view, we got what we were after.

The first thing we heard was a high-pitched cry. The farther we went, the louder the cries became, and the more there were.

As we made our way through the snowy mountain, a troop of Japanese macaques appeared in front of us.

A troop of Japanese macaques sat on the branches of a huge, leafless tree covered in snow.

The hostile troop of Japanese macaques kept crying kee-kee as they threatened a huge creature below the tree.

“Hey, Ori. Look over there.”

“I see it. Shh, be quiet.”

We crouched low and whispered to each other as we watched the commotion, our hearts pounding.

The creature the troop of Japanese macaques was threatening really was Bigfoot.

It was a humanoid about 3 m tall. Its huge, shaggy body was covered in gray fur, and its squat frame had especially large feet. Its distinct animal stink hit my nose, and the Blue Witch pinched her nose shut.

It was there.

Bigfoot is really there!

...But that excitement only lasted a moment.

As I watched the legendary creature with bated breath, I started to see what had happened.

The troop of Japanese macaques seemed to want to drive Bigfoot away. They cried kee-kee and shook the branches, dumping snow onto Bigfoot.

Meanwhile, Bigfoot had gotten snow dumped over its head. It twisted its ugly monkey face into an even uglier shape and drooped its shoulders.

As it let out deep, grating growls and gently reached a huge arm up toward the branches, a mother monkey holding a baby screamed and clawed at it.

“Oloron,” Bigfoot roared sadly. A gray Gremlin glinted on the tip of its nose, telling us what Bigfoot really was.

“Ori. This is...”

“Yeah. It's a monkey that mutated into a monster.”

The owner of the footprints hadn't been Bigfoot.

It was a monkey that had mutated into a monster. It may have looked worthy of the name Bigfoot, but it wasn't a creature that had always looked like that.

Even though it no longer looked like its companions, it was still trying to return to their troop, only to be rejected.

S-sad. Is there even a monster this sad?

Don't do this to me. I'm the kind of guy who watches some sappy animal documentary and bawls his eyes out over it. Even if it is a hairy, stinky, huge monkey, seeing it look that sad makes my chest hurt.

“...Maybe I'm still pretty lucky. Even though I'm no longer human, I can still live in human society.”

“Thaaat's heavy. That's got way too much painful experience behind it.”

The Blue Witch was hurting for a different reason than I was.

We came to look for a cute, fluffy white wolf, and this is one hell of a surprise. This isn't what I wanted to see.

As my mood darkened, thick, gloomy clouds hung low overhead as if to pile on, and the snow finally started coming down hard.

Then the melancholy scene took a turn.

A white wolf emerged from the heavy snow.

The white wolf hurried to Bigfoot's side and rubbed its muzzle against Bigfoot's thick leg.

Then it planted itself proudly on all fours beside Bigfoot and barked fiercely at the openly hostile troop of monkeys crying kee-kee.

An angry roar echoed through the snowy mountains.

When the wolf barked louder than all of them put together, the monkeys immediately panicked and scattered.

The white wolf that had driven the monkeys away gave a victorious howl at the sky. A blue Gremlin, just as the Blue Witch had said, was embedded in its forehead.

Bigfoot, its shoulders still drooping, gave the white wolf a single stroke as it nestled close to offer comfort, then trudged off into the white landscape beyond the heavy snow.

The two shadows disappeared beyond the winter.

And so the monsters' little scene on the snowy mountain ended with a small measure of relief amid the sadness.

“I see. So monsters live in symbiosis too.”

“Huh?”

When I summed up the unusual thing we'd just seen and muttered in admiration, the Blue Witch made a sound like she'd heard an unfamiliar foreign language.

Huh? The Blue Witch doesn't understand symbiosis? You used to be a high school girl. Did you even pay attention in biology class?

“The word symbiosis means living things of different species living together and affecting each other. When both sides benefit, that's mutualism. When only one side benefits, that's commensalism. When one side benefits while making the other suffer, that's parasitism. Going by what we just saw, the white wolf helped Bigfoot, so at least it wasn't parasitism, and—”

“Wait, wait, wait. I know what symbiosis is. That's not it. That's not what I mean. Why do you have to make things complicated like that? Those two were friends. That's all you need to know, right?”

“Huh?”

The Blue Witch cut me off, and this time it was my turn to make a sound like I'd heard an alien language.

Friends? That mutt and that ape? There's even a saying that dogs and monkeys don't get along. No way.

“Looking like you get along and actually getting along are two different things. Say you take a new hire along to introduce them to a client. Even if you think the new hire is useless trash, you'd put on a fake smile and pretend you got along, right? Would you look at that and think, Oh, this new hire and that person are good friends? No, right? Friendship is a pure relationship that exists beyond necessity, interests, and keeping up appearances. As long as I can't rule out the possibility that Bigfoot and the white wolf, living in the wild, were helping each other because necessity compelled them to raise each other's chances of survival, and were following a code of conduct that avoided upsetting the other and maximized their own benefit, it's premature to decide those two were friends.”

After I laid out my theory, the Blue Witch let out her biggest sigh of the day.

Come on. Did I say anything wrong?

“Ugh, fine, fine. I'll listen to your annoying friendship theory all you want. Now let's hurry down the mountain. The blizzard's setting in.”

The Blue Witch held my hand and led me down the mountain trail so we wouldn't get separated in the growing whiteout. Snow this heavy was rare even in Okutama.

We'd wound up witnessing some unexpected monster ecology, and I had plenty to say about that, but we'd also gotten to see the white wolf the Blue Witch had told me about.

Well, it wasn't a bad outing.
