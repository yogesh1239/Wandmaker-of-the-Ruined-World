Okyaku, who had come from the Tohoku Hunting Association, brought three new technologies to Tokyo.

One was secret sauce. A magic sauce that made monster meat, which you couldn't eat boiled or grilled, edible.

Another was the Monster Trap. It was a time-stasis trap magic tool, and I'd put it to use in Himori Wand.

And the last was a monster-material processing method.

Normally, monsters began deteriorating as materials immediately after death. As time passed, their magic power drained away, making them lose their magical properties and grow brittle and weak. It depended on the material, but within a month at most, all their magic power would be gone and they would become unusable.

What prevented that magic-power deterioration was the self-blood tanning developed by the Tohoku Hunting Association.

Between eating meat, magic traps, and material processing, the veterans in Tohoku really were pursuing magic technology specialized for hunting. If Tokyo's technological system was on the wizard side, Tohoku's was on the hunter side. The technology exchange was getting a whole lot done, which was great.

For the past month, I'd been making Himori Wand, messing around with the Monster Trap, and making a big barrel for soaking things in homemade secret sauce. At last, I had wrapped all of that up and could start on self-blood tanning.

“All right.”

I put on gloves, had a big stretch, and looked up at the sky.

Self-blood tanning had a lot of pretty bloody steps, so I did all the work in the yard rather than the workshop. The weather that day was cloudy. The rainy season was still a little ways off, there was no sign of rain, and it looked like it would be a cool, comfortable workday.

This time, I would process a rabbit monster that had been caught in a boar trap. Judging from the rigor mortis, it seemed to have been dead for about two hours.

I put on a work apron in case of blood splatter, sat down on the blue tarp I had spread out, and got to work right away.

First, I cut an artery without damaging the hide as much as possible, hung it upside down, and collected its blood in a washbasin I'd prepared.

While I waited for the blood to drip out, I dug out the Gremlin, ground it into as fine a powder as possible with a mortar and pestle, then sprinkled the finished Gremlin powder into the washbasin of blood and stirred it lightly to mix it evenly.

Then I butchered the bled-dry monster carcass and separated the materials I needed from the ones I didn't. This time, I didn't need any of the meat or organs. All I needed were the hide and bones.

After quickly cutting it apart and stripping off the hide and bones, I submerged them in the solution of Gremlin and blood... They wouldn't all go under. There wasn't enough liquid. Even with weights on top, part of them still stuck up above the surface.

Just in case, I reread the manual and checked the procedure, reviewing what to do in a situation like this.

“Uh, step three... If the materials you want to process cannot be fully submerged... dilute the solution with saline up to twice the original liquid volume... If they still cannot be fully submerged, reduce the amount of material until they can... Okay, okay. Saline.”

I had all the tools I needed ready. I opened a plastic bottle of saline that I had made beforehand and poured it into the washbasin. The materials immediately sank completely into the pool of blood. That did it.

The prep work was done, so I set the washbasin on the fire pit and lit a fire. There wasn't that much in the washbasin, so it came to a boil in about ten minutes.

Once I confirmed it was boiling, I took the washbasin off the heat and let it cool naturally.

Once the temperature dropped to about room temperature, the processing would be almost finished. All I had to do then was take the materials out of the self-blood solution, wash off the blood with water, and dry them.

Hmm. That had been easier than I expected. It wasn't a difficult job I needed to brace myself for. It was pretty bloody, though.

Since I had nothing to do until it finished cooling, I flipped through the Tohoku Hunting Association's book of secret techniques (a copied booklet compiled by Tokyo Magic University from Mr. Okyaku's oral teachings).

The more I read, the more I thought that how people adapted and responded to the age of magic brought by the Gremlin Disaster really showed the character of the land, or maybe the people.

Maybe because I had been driving technological development in Tokyo after getting inspired by Okutameteorite, magic-wand technology had advanced here. Thanks to Professor Ohinata, magic linguistics had advanced too.

Tohoku, on the other hand, was led by a hardcore hunting group originally formed by actual licensed hunters, so its technological development had gone in that direction too.

Processing monster meat was exactly the kind of thing hunters would do, and the Monster Trap was plainly inspired by a bear trap. Self-blood tanning was also close to traditional brain tanning.

It made sense that magic-wand technology had not developed in Tohoku. Self-blood tanning used a monster's Gremlin by smashing it to pieces. To the Tohoku crowd, Gremlins were things to break, crush, and put to use. That was fundamentally different from Tokyo's magic-wand technology, which prized bigger chunks of Gremlin and larger magic stones.

If there were this many differences in technology trees in Tohoku, how different would places farther away be? If it was different even within Japan, what about overseas? Brazil or America? It wouldn't be strange if completely unknown magic-technology systems that no one in Tokyo had ever imagined were already established there.

That was only if they had overcome the Gremlin Disaster, though...

As far as the Dragon Witch had found while flying around the Japanese archipelago, Japan had five known major survivor communities that had managed to maintain civilization properly.

Hokkaido's “Hokkaido Magic Beast Farm.”

Tohoku's “Tohoku Hunting Association.”

Tokyo's “Tokyo Witches' Council.”

Shiga's “Lake Biwa Pact.”

Kyushu's “Arataki Group.”

Those were the only five known. No one knew anything at all about the situation overseas.

Now that electricity could no longer be used, civilization must have suffered enormous regression and chaos worldwide. How well had countries around the world managed to cope?

It was entirely possible that humanity's survivors had happened to concentrate in Japan, leaving it as Earth's only remaining civilization.

Even Japan would have been wiped out by the mushroom pandemic if not for the Flower Witch. Similar follow-up magical disasters might have happened overseas too...

At some point, I had stopped turning the pages of the Tohoku Hunting Association's book of secret techniques and gotten lost in thought. The washbasin had cooled completely.

Taking the monster materials out of the cooled blood solution and rinsing them with well water revealed a beautiful white pelt and white bones.

Yep, they looked good. I couldn't tell whether I had processed them properly, since I was inexperienced.

Apparently, Transcendents could somehow tell processed monster materials from unprocessed ones through their sense for magic power, even without learning anything in particular. Those guys were unfair. I wanted that kind of magic-power sense too.

But there was no point wishing for what I didn't have. I hung the damp monster materials in a shady, well-ventilated spot under the eaves. Then I took a bath, got the smell of blood off, and went right to bed.

They should be dry by morning. I can't wait to see how they turn out...

---

Secret sauce, a new technology passed down from the Tohoku Hunting Association, revolutionized the dinner table.

Since monster meat that had always been thrown away could now be eaten, Tokyo's protein supply improved dramatically. Meat production, which had temporarily fallen due to the mushroom pandemic, grew even more active than before, and my dinner table, which had been a little lonely lately, became lavish again.

I even ate five homemade monster-meat sausages first thing in the morning. I added some asparagus from the yard on the side too, so the nutritional balance was good. When I shoveled it down with white rice, its rich flavor soaked through my whole body. Eating meat really made me feel powerful.

After finishing that great breakfast, I opened the storm shutters with my toothbrush in my mouth. I checked the monster materials drying in the shade by touch and found they had dried nicely.

The magical properties of the materials I could get from this rabbit monster were, frankly, nothing special. The fur just never tangled. The bones just made a sound like glass when you hit them.

That was exactly why I wouldn't mind ruining them. They were perfect materials for a self-blood-tanning beginner.

To commemorate the first monster materials I had processed myself, I put the pelt and bones up on the workshop wall as trophies. Yep, yep. My workshop was getting pretty well stocked, wasn't it? I wanted to improve both its usefulness and its looks.

While I worshipped Okutameteorite and adjusted the tension of the pelt, a short message came from the Blue Witch through an eyeball familiar.

When I went out through the front door, the Blue Witch emerged from beyond the thin Lost Mist spread across Okutama.

The Blue Witch was casually pulling a large dump truck loaded with monster carcasses, gripping its tow rope in one hand.

“Morning, Ori. I brought them.”

“Y-Yeah. That's incredible.”

She was so absurdly powerful that I gave a strained laugh. Why is she moving a large vehicle so easily? What kind of power is that? Even walking a dog takes more effort than that.

“Where do I put it?”

“Uh, just park it on the street for now. I'm working in the yard, so pull it up close to the yard.”

“Got it.”

The Blue Witch easily pulled the large vehicle over and parked it by the yard as if it were a tricycle, then set the parking brake in the driver's seat.

I'd only asked her to load some unprocessed monster materials onto a cart and bring them over, but I hadn't expected her to use a 10-ton dump truck as the cart. Should I praise her as a witch, or fear her as human heavy machinery?

“Sorry as always. I keep asking you to handle all kinds of hassles.”

“It's fine. I do it because I want to. You asked me, Ori. What are you using this for?”

“Of course I'm using it to make magic wands.”

“Again? That's all you ever do, Ori.”

“That's a good thing.”

The best craftsmen never stop honing their skills.

Or what? Am I supposed to coast on my natural dexterity and existing technology, slack off, and turn into an outdated fossil? Huh? That wouldn't be good, right!

“Aren't you interested in butchering monsters, Blue Witch?”

“Hmm... Even if they're monsters, I don't like handling corpses. I don't like the smell of blood either. I don't feel like it. I think it's amazing that you don't flinch at this stuff, Ori.”

That little bit of flattery felt pretty good. Heh heh heh. Well, maybe I am amazing.

Even in this hard age, with death far closer than it had been in the Reiwa era[^1], plenty of people probably had trouble cutting up and processing corpses.

Personally, I thought the Blue Witch's resolve to take a human life without hesitation if needed was more amazing, though.

The Blue Witch had, as ordered, brought only tough monsters and slime-type monsters. Together, we unloaded them from the dump truck's bed into the yard.

The Blue Witch pushed a soft, translucent, jellyfish-like slime carcass off the truck bed with the tip of her foot, then rubbed the sticky stuff from her shoe onto the tarp with a disgusted look.

“Can something like this really be used for a wand? Its Gremlin is small, and it doesn't look usable for handle material either.”

“It can, it can. Slime-type monsters are soft when they're alive, but I hear they get hard once they die and their magic power drains out.”

“Is that so?”

“Ah, you didn't know?”

I'd assumed she knew, but unexpectedly, the Blue Witch shook her head. A witch who killed monsters nonstop seemed like she'd know a lot about this stuff.

“No. Until now, when I killed a monster, I only took out the Gremlin, then burned or buried it.”

“Ah.”

Of course. Until now, the only monster materials Tokyo had used were Gremlins.

“Well, I only know secondhand too. But apparently slime gets hard like glass when all its magic power drains out, so I thought it could be used as a void filler for wand cores.”

“I see...?”

The Blue Witch nodded vaguely. She didn't seem to get it.

This isn't someone else's problem. This could lead to an update patch for Cyanos too.

Multilayer construction weakened magic-wand cores like Cyanos's. Since it involved carving one sphere inside another, a gap formed between the outer and inner spheres, making the core fragile.

Right now, I was filling that gap with acrylic resin to increase its strength, but that stuff was a problem.

Acrylic resin lasted about 5–7 years. On top of that, new production had stopped completely after civilization collapsed, and even the small remaining supply we had was nearing the end of its lifespan. The acrylic resin used in Cyanos would start deteriorating in another 2–3 years, cracking, yellowing, losing its transparency, and falling apart.

This is a seriously big problem.

If I only thought about practicality, I could overlook it. But it would seriously damage a magic wand's appearance.

Unacceptable. Seriously.

Just imagining Cyanos-chan aging and deteriorating over time made me want to tear at my hair. I have to do something.

That was why I had my eye on slime material. Apparently the Tohoku Hunting Association shaped and used it in place of glass, and it showed no sign of deteriorating even after three years.

I didn't know how long slime material would last, but unlike acrylic resin, which could not be produced again, it could be obtained and replaced indefinitely. That was huge.

Slime material hardened once its magic power drained regardless of individual differences, so I had the Blue Witch pick the weakest carcasses with the least magic power—the ones whose magic power would drain most easily. I squeezed and filtered one of them to get a viscous, nearly transparent liquid.

After that, all I had to do was borrow Cyanos for a moment, scrape out the acrylic-resin filler with a hooked tool, carefully pour in the slime solution without letting bubbles get into it, and seal it up.

I quickly finished the whole job, gave Cyanos back, and asked what she thought.

“How is it? Did the weight change? If there's anything you want adjusted, tell me. Even if it's something tiny.”

“Mm... No, it has barely changed. As always, good work.”

The Blue Witch lightly shook Cyanos and checked how it felt. She looked satisfied.

Excellent. Then the upgrade from Cyanos Ver. 2.2 to Cyanos Ver. 2.3 is complete without a hitch.

Little updates like this might be a pain for the user, but as the maker, I wanted to keep doing them regularly. Maintenance is important.

“I sealed it, so it shouldn't leak. But don't swing it around hard for about a week, until the solution has properly hardened.”

“Got it.”

The Blue Witch nodded obediently. Usually, the Blue Witch tended to nag me about being tactless, socially awkward, lacking a sense of danger, and whatever else. But when it came to magic wands, she trusted me and was easy to deal with.

That made me a little happy. Sometimes people used the things I made for unintended purposes... Man, it was great to have a customer who used her magic wand for its intended purpose, followed the precautions, and didn't use it as a brace, club, or shield.

Once I handed over a product, it was up to the user whether they used it roughly. But it still felt good when they took care of it.

I was glad my friend was the kind of person who took care of her wand. I heard the Setagaya Witch kept leaving her wand lying around, snapping it, and breaking it. I didn't really want to make wands for someone like that.

“From now on, I'll use slime solution for multilayer wand-core filler, so I'd appreciate it if you'd bring me some regularly. What you brought this time will be enough for a while, though.”

“All right. I'll bring you slime. You also said to bring tough monsters. What are those for?”

“I'm planning to use them for tools.”

“Tools.”

The Blue Witch looked over at the stone-like and metal-like monster carcasses in the dump truck's bed and put a hand to her chin in thought.

“I often hear about turning monster materials into weapons and armor. But tools...”

“When I'm doing fine processing on Gremlins or magic stones, I use finely sharpened hook needles and stuff. But they don't have enough hardness or strength, so man, does it take time. You know how monsters are ridiculously tough, right? Even thin little hairs like thread can be as hard as steel. I think it would make things go a lot faster if I could make fine-detail tools out of sturdy monster materials imbued with magic power.”

“I see. If you say so, Ori, then I guess so.”

“I call them magic tools!”

“You just wanted to say that.”

Her voice was exasperated, but the Blue Witch was smiling. Come on, starting with the name matters too. I know you like the stylish name “Dragon-Furnace-Carved Seven-Layer Blue Wand Cyanos” after all.

Monster materials that I had not been able to use until now could be used now. From here on, they would surely let me get at all kinds of things that had been just out of reach.

I'm the guy who repurposes every technology into magic-wand making. I'll keep evolving magic wands forever.
